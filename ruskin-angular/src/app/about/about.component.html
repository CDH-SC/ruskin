John Ruskin was born into a wealthy trade family; the money was made from selling sherry to the great houses of Great Britain. He was not sent to a public school—Eton, Harrow, Charterhouse, for example—but largely educated at home through private tutors. He proceeded as a Gentleman Commoner to Christ Church Oxford where he read for a degree in the then customary mixture of Classics and mathematics. He began publishing on architecture very early in his life—The Poetry of Architecture (1837), for instance—but first made his mark with the initial volume of Modern Painters (1843), a magnificent defense of J. M. W. Turner. There would be five volumes, ending in 1860, overall.

Ruskin wrote prolifically throughout his long career. His most notable achievements included The Stones of Venice (1851–3), Unto this Last (1860), and the feisty political letters, Fors Clavigera (1871–84). His correspondence was voluminous, and he lectured, taught, and wrote with a seemingly boundless energy. He lost his Evangelical faith at the end of the 1850s—his brief marriage to Effie Gray had ended earlier—and the 1860s were characterized by exploratory writing and thinking, in new genres and on new topics. He returned to a more syncretic, non-doctrinaire Christianity in the middle of the 1870s. In 1869, Ruskin was appointed as the first Slade Professor of Fine Art at the University of Oxford, though he quickly became disenchanted with the aspirations of the undergraduates. His books in this later period included work on Florence, Greek myth, engraving, Amiens, and a new book on the history of Venice.

Mental illness dogged Ruskin’s last creative years and, well into his autobiography Praeterita (1885–9), he suffered a serious breakdown from which he did not recover. His last decade was spent more or less in silence at Brantwood, his Lake District home at Coniston.

Technical Team


The John Ruskin Archive was created by a student project team at the Center for Digital Humanities at the University of South Carolina (https://sc.edu/about/centers/digital_humanities/). Initial development was done by Tyron Schultz, Ian McDowell, Caleb Kitzmann, and Prithvi Tippabhatla in 2018. They have subsequently been joined by Stella Masucci and Mitchell Lambert. The project has been supported by systems administration and development operations team members Joshua Nelson, Zach Stump, and Dan Rochester. This and other projects of the Victorian Lives and Letters Consortium (https://vllc.wordpress.cdhsc.org/) have been managed in the past four years by Morgan Lundy and now Helen B. Kampmann Marodin. Prof. Dr. Colin Wilder is the project director at USC and technical lead of the VLLC.



This site is under construction using the MEAN stack (Mongo Database, Express.js, Angular.js, Node.js) and started as a clone of the Diaries of Michael Field in late summer of 2019 by Developers Ian Mcdowell and Tyron Schultz.

In future iterations of this site, we hope to include annotations, more letters of Ruskin, and a seamless integration of Ruskin’s letters with the Carlyles’ letters to ease reading of the correspondence in chronological order, among other planned features.
